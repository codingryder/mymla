# Civic Vellum

A visual philosophy for the MyMLA seal — and any companion artefact that needs
to feel like an artefact of public service rather than a logo.

## I. Manifesto

There is a difference between a brand and an emblem. A brand wants to be
remembered; an emblem wants to be trusted. *Civic Vellum* is the philosophy of
the trusted emblem — the slow, patient mark stamped on a ledger, the impression
left on warm sealing wax, the quiet authority of a document that does not need
to shout because it knows what it is.

Civic Vellum draws from a long lineage of municipal seals, university crests
and printer's marks, but strips away every ornament that fashion would have
added. What remains is the bare grammar of officialdom — a circle that holds,
a centre that anchors, a horizon that grounds, and lettering that arches
without straining. The composition is symmetrical, but never sterile. It
breathes.

## II. The mark, in spatial terms

The form is **a single concentric ring** with one whisper-thin gold ring set
just inside it — a hairline, the visual equivalent of a held breath. Within
this ring sits a **building**: not photographic, not particular, but
*typological*. A dome over a colonnade, a portico over a plinth. Geometry
recognisable as *civic* from thirty metres or at forty-eight pixels.

Beneath the building, **a single horizontal stroke** — a baseline, a horizon,
a desk on which the constituency rests. Around the upper arc, the Latin
script. Around the lower arc, the Malayalam script. The two languages mirror
each other but do not crowd. The mark is heavier at the bottom than the top —
gravity is part of the design.

## III. Material and palette

The teal is *not* peacock or aquamarine; it is the colour of monsoon-darkened
brass, of a riverstone held to the light. **One** teal, used everywhere the
ground appears. Against it, **one** warm ivory — the colour of old paper that
has not yellowed, only softened. The optional gold is *barely* gold: a single
hairline that the eye can decide is either metal or a trick of the printing.

There are no gradients except the subtlest darkening at the very edge of the
disc — a vignette so quiet it reads as depth rather than effect. There are no
textures imitating paper; if the philosophy is doing its job, the viewer
*imagines* the paper. Nothing in the palette competes with the form.

## IV. Craft and rigour

Every element must look as if it has been engraved rather than drawn.
Strokes have a slight weight bias — heavier where a steel die would have
struck deepest. Curves are circular arcs, not bezier guesses; the letter-arcs
are positioned with a typographer's care so that the letters stand *radially*,
each one's spine pointed at the centre of the seal, not tilted to chase the
ring. The kerning is generous but not lax. The space between the text-ring
and the inner ring is consistent to within a pixel.

This is the discipline of an emblem-cutter, not a graphic designer. The
result must reward sustained inspection: zoom in and the corners are still
clean, the strokes still resolved, the symmetry still intact. Zoom out, and
the silhouette still reads as "official". Both states are non-negotiable.

## V. The hidden reference

Embedded — not announced — is the silhouette of the Kerala Niyamasabha
building in Thiruvananthapuram, abstracted just enough that it could equally
read as any colonial-revival assembly hall on the subcontinent. A reader who
has seen the building from Vellayambalam will feel it; a reader who has not
will simply read "legislature". This is the jazz quotation: a chord that
those who know will hear, and that everyone else will simply enjoy as music.

## VI. What this is for

The result is not decoration. It is identification. When this mark sits in
the small circle next to a WhatsApp message, the citizen who taps it should
feel — without being told — that they are speaking to an *office*, not a
broadcast, not a bot, not a campaign. The philosophy succeeds when the mark
disappears into that feeling, when the visual work itself becomes invisible
behind the small civic act it enables.
